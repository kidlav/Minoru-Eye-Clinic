Comprehensive Eye Exam

A comprehensive eye exam isn’t just about finding the right glasses prescription. Our optometrist ensures your eyes are healthy, free from disease, and working properly together. Regular eye exams are essential for everyone, even without noticeable vision changes, as many eye conditions develop without symptoms.
Adults aged 19 to 65 should have an eye exam every year unless advised otherwise by their doctor.

Vision is absolutely vital in the future success of children in school and in future career options. Exams for young ones start as early as 6 months. It is important to do the eye exam as early as possible as some eye conditions are harder to treat the older the child is. Don’t wait for your child to complain about his/her vision as most children don’t know what “normal” vision is like. Child exams include looking for any need for glasses, any eye turn and childhood eye diseases.

Parents should look for any eye turn while spending time with their children. For example signs of crossed eye while eating a meal or any eye turn while watching the TV. Bring your child in if you are suspicious of anything abnormal.

Vision plays a crucial role in maintaining independence and quality of life as we age. Regular eye exams are essential for detecting and managing age-related conditions such as cataracts, glaucoma, macular degeneration, and diabetic retinopathy. Many of these conditions develop gradually and may not show symptoms in the early stages, making routine check-ups even more important.

Seniors should be mindful of vision changes, such as difficulty reading, increased glare sensitivity, or trouble seeing at night. Family members should also watch for signs of declining vision, such as frequent squinting, difficulty recognizing faces, or increased clumsiness. If you or a loved one notice any of these signs, schedule an exam today to ensure continued eye health and well-being.

Diabetes can significantly impact eye health, often without noticeable symptoms in the early stages. A Diabetic Eye Exam is crucial for detecting and managing diabetic retinopathy, macular edema, glaucoma, and cataracts, all of which can lead to vision loss if left untreated.

High blood sugar levels can weaken the blood vessels in the eyes, causing bleeding, swelling, and oxygen deprivation to the retina. Since early detection is key to preventing vision loss, individuals with diabetes should have a comprehensive eye exam at least once a year, even if their vision seems unchanged.

If you have diabetes, protect your sight by scheduling regular eye exams to catch any issues early and maintain long-term vision health.
